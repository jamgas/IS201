The PhD Life

Salary

For Information Systems professors at top tier business schools the starting salary is currently (2021) around $120,000-$220,000 with an approximate 2x lifetime increase (dependent upon location). Moving often increases salary. Usually, professors are paid on 8-10 month contracts which can be annualized over a 12-month schedule. Salaries vary greatly by location and institution. However, a 2019 survey of assistant, associate, and full professors at public universities roughly on par with BYU (in terms of prestige) is visualized below. In general, private universities tend to pay slightly more, but their salaries are not publicly disclosed.

Job Outlook

The current job outlook for BYU PhD Prep students that go on to finish with a PhD in Information Systems is fabulous. At this time there are approximately 3 good jobs for every qualified candidate.

Getting the degree

  • Duration - The average time required to get a PhD is 4 years, but 5 is not uncommon. Some are able to get their PhD in 3 years.
  • Schedule - Your schedule is typically very flexible, but can depend on the program. The first two years are more structured, and the second two are more like being a professor - you get to decide how much, where, and what you work on.
  • Research - PhD students will research with an expert in the field who will act as their advisor. This provides the student with an opportunity to get connected with their vast network. Networking is critical and needs to be done at conferences all around the world! Researchers have opportunities to work with funded labs or grants, often supported by the government or related industry.

Working as a professor

  • Hours - Completely dependent upon you. How many hours per week do you currently work? The hours that you put into school and work currently will likely be similar throughout your career. How well do you delegate and prioritize? How well can you discard ideas and projects that are not going to add sufficient value merited by the work involved? Much like trying to guess how much time a certain class will require, there are a number of personal factors that will work into the equation. With those factors in mind, the histogram below is an estimated representation of the distribution of weekly workload during semesters (summers are often much less). Very few professors work less than 25 hours per week, and few work more than 60-70. The average workweek for a professor is estimated to be 35-45 hours. Obviously, some weeks are more or less.
  • Duties
    • Research - The PhD prepares you for research-oriented professor positions. Some universities prefer their professors to focus on research instead of teaching. If you become a research professor you will be expected to publish a couple of articles per year (depending on the rigor and prestige of the university). A teaching professor does not have research expectations. Researchers work to publish with reputable and prestigious scientists and conduct state-of-the-art, cutting edge science, all while working to expand the borders of knowledge in their field.
    • Teaching - Nearly all professors teach at least one class, though most, even those who are at research-focused institutions, teach around 4 courses per year. Heavier research focus means less teaching, whereas heavier teaching focus means less research.
    • Service - All professors are expected to provide local and global service. They should serve on university, college, and department committees. Professors should also provide service to the scientific community at large.
  • Full autonomy of schedule - As a professor you get to decide when you work, what you work on, how you do it, and where you do it. You have no boss, and never need to "clock-in" because you are measured on your performance and productivity rather than your time. As a professor there are no vacation days because you don't have to be in your office at all unless you are holding office hours. Even then, you get to determine when and where your office hours are!
  • Travel - Travel is part of most professional careers. Researching professors can travel as often or as infrequently as they would like. Most travel about twice per year to academic conferences where they present their most recent research to other professors. These conferences (for IS: ICIS, HICSS, AMCIS, ECIS, PACIS) are often held in exotic locations. Since joining BYU, I have been flown to Ireland, Italy, Germany, Korea, China, Puerto Rico, Hawaii, Denmark, New Zealand, Australia, Mexico, Canada, and all over the US. These trips are usually fully paid for by your university, and they often include enough downtime to thoroughly enjoy the location. Professors who do not want to travel do not need to. Professors also often travel for consulting engagements, which also provide another source of income.
